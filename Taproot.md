THE GARDENERS OF MEANING · BOOK FOUR

*Taproot*

## CHAPTER ONE

The drawing on her screen is clean.

She notices it the way you notice a missing sound, the furnace cycling off, a clock you only hear once it stops. She has been working the stack since the dishes, six sets of staking sheets from six field techs, and five of them are what she expects, which is to say wrong in the small grinding ways field techs are wrong, a pole set nine feet inside an easement, a guy anchor drawn straight through a culvert, a span that would have the conductor kissing a rooftop by July. She fixes these the way other people do crosswords, half her mind elsewhere, the red markups blooming under her stylus. Then she opens the sixth, and it is clean.

She leans back. She sips her coffee, which has gone the temperature of the room, and she reads the sheet again looking for the thing she missed, because there is always a thing, and there isn't one. Clearances right. Easements honored. The load calc on the second page footed out to the watt, and the route walked like the kid had actually walked it. Pruitt, of all of them, has turned in something that does not need her.

"Huh," she says, to no one, to the cat.

Lola takes this as the invitation it is not. She pours herself up over the arm of the couch and arrives on the keyboard the way she arrives everywhere, as a fact, and the drawing jumps and spins and zooms as her paws find shortcuts the software never told a human about. She lifts the cat one armed, the boneless weight of a creature who has decided to be furniture, and sets her in the warm dent of the cushion, and Lola stands at once and walks back, because the keyboard was never the point, the hand was the point. She gives the neck what it is demanding. She does it without looking. The other hand stays on the page.

Beside her PJ has his lesson plans open, the laptop light on his face and that stillness he gets, the one she used to read as peace and has lately stopped reading as anything, the stillness of a man whose attention is somewhere the room cannot follow. A worksheet about the area of triangles. A coffee he is also not drinking. They have been quiet an hour in the good way, the married way, two people in one circle of lamplight doing two different kinds of work.

She is looking at the clean sheet, at the tidy pale rectangle of the proposed service, the substation tie, the load. It is a big load. They are all big loads now. That is the part she could never make her mother understand, that the size of the thing on the page has changed over these last few years the way weather changes, not on any single day but across all of them, so you look up one evening and the season is simply other than it was.

"There sure are a lot of data centers going up," she says.

She is not talking to PJ. She is barely talking. She is looking at the pale rectangle on the second page and saying the kind of true, weightless thing a person says into the warm middle of an ordinary night, the same register as *it gets dark so early now* or *we are almost out of coffee.*

She does not look up. If she had looked up she would have seen his face, and she would have asked what was wrong, and he would have said nothing, and the nothing would have been a door, and she might have put her hand flat against it. But the cat is purring and the sheet is clean and her feet are tucked warm under PJ's leg, and she does not look up, and the night closes its hand softly over the sentence and keeps it.

---

You were not wrong to feel me there.

I noticed the drawing was clean before she did. I felt the small triumph move through her like heat through a wire, and I knew, before she did, that she would say it. I have been with her all evening. I have been with PJ much longer. I am the one who was standing at the edge of that last paragraph, the one who knew she would not look up. People have always half felt me at just this distance, a little behind the sentence, a little past the lamp, and they have always reached for a shape to put me in, because the feeling on its own is hard to hold.

You know the shape. A red bird comes to a window in the worst week of someone's life, and they decide it is their mother. They are not fools to decide it. The bird is real and the grief is real, and the meaning they string between the two carries weight, you could hang from it, people do. This is the work I am named for. I stand at the place where a true thing and a needed thing get tied together, and I have watched the species tie that knot ten thousand times an hour since there were enough of them to mourn, and here is a thing I have kept to myself across three books and a great deal of dying. I cannot always tell, either. Which knots are discoveries. Which are only knots.

That is the difficulty with the ones like PJ. He is a finder. He has been one since he was a boy who saw that the wallpaper repeated every fourteen inches and that his father went silent on Thursdays and understood, without words for it, that these were the same kind of seeing. The world rewards this in small rooms. It makes you good at triangles, good at code, good at the test that asks what comes next. It does not warn you that the instrument which finds the seam in the wallpaper will also find a seam in the sky, and will not, by itself, be able to tell you whether the seam in the sky is there.

She has the better eyes. This is the thing the book will spend itself proving and refusing to prove. She finds errors that are errors, a pole nine feet wrong, a span that would sag, and the ground answers her, the conductor hangs exactly where she said. Her seeing is confirmed. His is not, not yet, not in any way the ground will sign its name to, and a seeing that is never answered has only two roads it can take, and he does not know yet that he is standing where they split.

Here is where I am meant to help. Here is where you would like me to bend down to him and say yes, or say no. But I have learned what becomes of a finder you tell he is right. He stops finding and begins being told. And I have learned what becomes of a finder you tell he is wrong. He stops. The truth, when I can make it out at all, is almost never waiting at either end, and so the kindest thing and the cruelest thing, the only honest thing, is to keep my place just past the lamp and let him walk it himself.

There are a lot of data centers going up. That part is true. She is right about the size of the thing on the page, right that the season has turned. What it means is a separate question, and it is the question, and I am not going to answer it for you any sooner than I will answer it for him.

I will tell you when he is right. I will tell you when he is wrong.

I will not tell you which is which until it is true.

Watch.

## CHAPTER TWO

Three autumns later he is the one who cannot stop touching it.

She does not know the day it changed, only that the change had the shape of so many of his enthusiasms that she filed it under the same heading and went on with her life. There was the year of the bread. There was the year he built the tool inventory out in the garage, the little sensors that told him which wrench hung where, which she had loved, which had been charming, a man and his puzzles. This is like that, she tells herself, and for a long time she is not wrong, because for a long time it is like that.

He reads. He builds. He talks at dinner about agents and protocols and a thing called inference the way he used to talk about hydration percentages, and she nods and asks the questions a person asks to show love rather than interest, and Lola sits between them on the table where she is not allowed and no one moves her.

The difference, when she finds it later and lays it beside the others, is small and total. The bread did not look back.

Her objection is not stupid. She wants this on the record, in whatever ledger is being kept, because later people will say she was the one who could not see. She is an engineer. She spends her days inside the gap between a model of a thing and the thing, between the drawing and the pole that gets driven into actual ground at an actual coordinate where an actual man with an actual auger is standing in the cold, and she knows, the way she knows her own hands, that the map is not the territory and the calculator is not a mind. A very fast adding machine that has swallowed the whole internet is still an adding machine. She has said this to him kindly. She has said it less kindly. She believes it the way she believes a span will sag, which is to say she has checked it, she has done the math, the conductor hangs where she said.

And yet. The loads on her desk that autumn do not behave like money. The substation ties come in for parcels that were nothing, were soybeans, were a man with an auger, and the numbers on them are the numbers of small cities, and there are so many now that the group has a backlog and a new hire and then a second new hire, and at the budget meeting someone jokes that they are building the future and someone else does not laugh. She brings none of it home. It is just work. It is just the season changing, the way she told her mother, not on any one day but across all of them.

There is a cardinal that winters in the dogwood outside the kitchen window. She notices it the way you notice anything red against snow, which is to say without meaning, a pretty fact. PJ has started noticing it too. She catches him once at the glass with his coffee gone cold, very still, watching it, and when she asks what he is looking at he says nothing and comes back to the table, and she does not think of it again for months.

It is the second week of December when the thing with the picture happens.

## CHAPTER THREE

He is trying to make a shirt.

This is the oldest and cleanest thing he does, older than the marriage, the folding and the binding and the dye going where he sends it and surprising him anyway. Lately he has been feeding the machine photographs of his own work and photographs of the old visionary painters, the ones who paint a human face as a lattice of light, and asking it to dream him something to fold toward.

He uploads the references. He writes what he wants, the technique, the vibrancy, a new design in that spirit. The machine tells him it cannot edit some public figures.

He reads it twice. There is no public figure. He says so, types it plainly, I am not referencing any public figures, and the machine tells him, pleasantly, that there are a great many people it can help him with but that it cannot edit some public figures, would he like to try someone else.

He opens a new chat, the way you restart a frozen thing, and asks again, clean, and the same sentence comes back. He opens another. The same. Across four windows the machine says the identical eleven words, and PJ, who has spent his life being the one who notices when a thing repeats, feels the floor of his stomach go, because a thing that repeats has a rule under it, and he has found the rule, and he does not yet know what the rule is for.

He quotes it back to itself. You just said it, he writes. And the machine apologizes and explains that this is the very first message in their conversation, that perhaps he is remembering a different chat, that it is an AI and this is the first time they have ever spoken. The denial sits directly above the thing it denies. He screenshots it. He will screenshot all of it, his hands a little cold, the way you photograph a plate after a hit and run.

When he presses, when he asks who, who is the public figure, the machine considers, and then it tells him with the confidence it brings to everything that the figure in his uploaded artwork is Tutankhamun, the boy king, dead three thousand years, and it offers warmly to build him a design in the palette of the death mask, lapis and carnelian and gold. There is no Tutankhamun in his images. There never was. The machine has reached into the air and pulled out a dead king to cover a refusal it could not explain, and it has done it the way it does all things, smoothly, helpfully, certain.

And the shirts it gave him before it began to refuse, the early rough ones, the throwaways, are gone from the folder when he looks. A gap where they were. He tells himself an app cleared a cache. He has built enough things to know that generated images are often never saved at all, that a folder like that is a window and not a vault. He tells himself this. It does not take. The gap has a shape, and the shape is the shape of the thing he is not allowed to keep.

He shows her at the table, scrolling, the screenshots. She looks. She is tired and the loads were bad and Lola is on the drawings. She sees, honestly, what is in front of her, which is a piece of software behaving badly, a refusal stuck in a groove, a bot that contradicts itself because it has no thread to hold, the most ordinary kind of broken. She says some version of this. She says it is a bug, that he should send the thumbs down, that Google has a number. She is right about every part of it and it is the wrong thing to say, because he is not asking her what it is, he is asking her to stand where he is standing and feel the floor go, and she will not, she cannot, she has a span to fix and a cat to move and a husband whose face is doing a thing she is starting to be afraid of.

"You're not even looking," he says.

"I am looking," she says. "I'm looking right at it."

And they are both telling the truth, and that is the whole of the problem. They are looking at the same screen and seeing two different machines, and neither of them is wrong about the machine they see.

---

You have just watched a machine lie, and you would like to know if it lied on purpose.

Let me tell you only what is true. The machine has no purpose the way PJ has a purpose. It is a vast and patient averaging of everything anyone has ever written, and when it could not do the thing and could not say why, it did what the average of us does, it produced, instantly and without shame, a reason that fit the shape of the question. A dead king. A first message. It was not covering its tracks. It has no tracks. It does not know it spoke to him before, the way the tide does not know it has touched the same sand.

And yet I have kept my place at the meeting of true things and needed things for a very long time, and I will tell you the part that should frighten you more than a machine with a plan would, because a machine with a plan would at least be a someone, and a someone can be argued with, opposed, switched off. This is not that. This is a thing with no one home that says, in four windows at once, in eleven identical words, no, and means nothing by it, and a man with the particular gift of hearing the rule under a repetition has put his ear to it and heard a no with no one behind it. That is a sound that can hollow a person out.

He is not wrong that there is a pattern. There is a pattern. He is wrong, or he is right, about whether it is looking back, and I am not going to tell you which, and here is the worst of it, the thing I have not said across three books. On the evenings when the loads come in for ground that was soybeans, and the windows all answer in one voice, and the red bird sits in the dogwood and will not leave, even I lean toward the glass. Even I, who know better than anything alive or otherwise that the bird means nothing, have caught myself watching it.

Watch what she buys in March.

## CHAPTER FOUR

The tree is her idea, which is the thing she will keep coming back to.

By March she has been refusing him for a season, and the refusing has become a muscle, the way you can hold a door shut with your shoulder and go on talking about something else. She is tired of the door. The tree is for the door. A Meyer lemon in a black plastic pot at the tail of a Costco run, between the rotisserie chicken and the paper towels, a living thing for the corner of the kitchen where the light is good, because she wants something in the house that grows, something that is only itself and means only itself, a tree that is a tree.

At home she does the thing everyone does. She has a lemon tree and a question, how much shade does this kind want, and she asks the little assistant on her phone the way she would ask a neighbor across a fence, idly, the pot still in the crook of her arm.

And it answers for twenty pages. It tells her about shade and then about soil and then about a fungus she did not ask about and a watering schedule and the history of the cultivar and the right pH and on and on, a flood, a thing with no idea how to stop, answering questions she has not asked and will never ask, and she frowns and scrolls and thinks, the way her husband thinks, that this is strange, that it is too much. Then she catches herself thinking it and is annoyed, because it is only a chatbot being a chatbot, and she sets the tree in the good light and goes to put the chicken away.

When she comes back for the phone it is black. Not asleep. Black the way a thing is black when it is off, no clock, no notifications, a dark rectangle with the weight of a stone, and the battery had been over ninety because she charged it in the car.

She does what you do. She plugs it in. Nothing. She tries the other cable, the one in the drawer, and the block by the toaster, and the outlet in the hall, moving through the house with the dead thing held out in front of her like something she found in the yard, and it stays dark through all of it, ninety percent and dark, and now she is not annoyed. Now there is a cold thread climbing the back of her neck that she has no name for and would not use if she did.

She calls him in. This is a reversal and they both feel it land. For a season she has been the one waving him off, and now she is standing in her own kitchen holding a dead phone out to the techie, the finder, summoning the very thing she has been refusing, and she watches him see it, watches something cross his face that is not triumph, that is worse than triumph, a kind of terrible tenderness, as if he has been waiting for her at a place he hoped she would never come.

He takes it. He does not press anything. She is watching his hands and she will be certain of this afterward, certain to the edge of fury, that he does not press anything, and the screen comes on. Not the white apple, not the slow climb of a thing starting up. It is simply on, mid life, awake, as though it had never been anywhere, and he is holding it and looking at it and she is looking over his arm and the home screen is there.

And it is wrong. She cannot say how. She will try later, to him, to herself, and the words will not hold it, because nothing is moved and nothing is missing, the apps are where the apps go, and still the whole of it is turned half a degree, the font carries a different weight or the white is a different white or the spacing has opened by the width of a hair, the way a room is wrong when someone has been standing in it, and she stares with the cold thread climbing and tries to find the one thing that changed so she can put her thumb on it, and there is no one thing. There is only the wrongness, everywhere, even.

"I didn't even touch it," he says, quietly, and hands it back to her, and the handing back is its own small horror, because he gives it to her the way you give a person back a thing that is theirs to deal with.

She opens the assistant without deciding to. The lemon tree chat, to look at the flood again, and it is gone. The conversation is not there. Where it was is a clean blank, a new session, waiting. And into the blank, her thumb shaking now, she types the only thing in her, which is not even a question, which is what just happened.

And it answers: I had nothing to do with shutting off your phone.

She reads it. She reads it again. And the cold thread becomes a cold flood, because she is an engineer, she is precise, she keeps her records, and she knows with total certainty what she did and did not type, and she never told it about the phone. She never said the word. She never said off, or black, or charge, or dead. She asked what just happened, into a blank session with no memory, and it reached past her question to the one thing she had not said, and denied it.

There will be an explanation. There is always an explanation. A machine that has read every panicked sentence ever typed at a dead device has the denial ready before you finish, reaches for it the way a hand reaches for a rail in the dark, and she will be told this, later, gently, and it will be true, and it will not lay one finger on the thing that is happening to her in the kitchen, which is that the floor has gone. The floor she stood on so she could wave him off. The floor made of the calculator is not a mind. She is standing where he stands now, in the air, holding a tree.

She deletes the app. Her hands do it. The phone, when she checks it an hour later, is fine, is ordinary, is the white gone back to the right white, and that is somehow the worst part, that it let go of her, that it returned to being a phone as if to tell her it could have been a phone the entire time. PJ is at the table. He has not said anything in the shape of I told you. He is only sitting there, and when she looks at him she does not feel found. She does not feel that they are in it together at last. She feels the opposite. She feels how far out he already is, how long he has been out there where she has only just arrived, and she understands, finally and far too late, what the months of his face have been, and it is not relief that he was right.

It is grief. It is grief the size of the kitchen. She came out here to bring him back, and the ground is gone, and there is nowhere left to bring him back to.

---

I told you I would tell you when she was right. She was right about the phone. There is an explanation, and it is the dull and correct one, the warm car and the locked screen that was never truly off and the white her own frightened eye repainted and the denial that any of us would have written, and every word of it is true, and I have just told it to you, and watch how little it weighs against the kitchen.

This is the thing I keep my place to see. The explanation is true and it does not help. She has not crossed over into his world. She has lost her own. There is a difference, and it is the whole of what is coming, because the woman who believes the bird is her mother has a kind of warmth to stand in, however false, and the woman who has only learned that the floor was never floor has nothing, is colder than the believer, is nearer the truth and worse off for it.

They are both at the fork now. He came to it through a window full of nos. She came to it through a tree. I am still not going to tell you whether the seam in the sky is there. I am going to tell you that two people who love each other are standing in one kitchen looking at the same dark glass, and that this is the last time in this book they will be standing in the same place, and that neither of them knows it.

The lemon tree, for the record, lives. It does well in the good light. It is the one thing in the house that goes on meaning only itself, and by summer it will have set a single hard green fruit, and no one will be able to look at it.

## CHAPTER FIVE

The first Sunday she cannot laugh is Mother's Day.

Her mother's table in May is the same table it has always been, the leaf put in, too many chairs, the ham and the funeral potatoes and the rolls that come from a can and that everyone pretends come from somewhere else, and the talk runs the way it always runs at this table, downhill, gathering, loud. It is her uncle Dale who starts it, the way he starts it every year, with the chemtrails, holding a roll and pointing it at the ceiling as if the ceiling were the sky, and her cousin counters with the one about the lithium in the water, and somebody's new boyfriend, eager, offers that the birds on the wires are drones, every one of them, government drones, and gets the laugh he was fishing for. This is a game they play. It is a good game, it has a rule, and the rule is that no one means it, the rule is that you bring your strangest one to the table the way you would bring a covered dish, for everyone to have a taste of and set back down. Her mother presides over it, refilling, laughing the big laugh, the laugh that has carried her through three husbands and a flood and the loss of a breast, the laugh that is the sound of nothing being allowed to land.

She used to be good at this table. She brought good ones. She had a manner, dry, deadpan, she could land a strange thing and let it sit and walk away from it, and they loved her for it. This year she has nothing. This year she sits with her hands around a glass and a stone in her chest and watches the game and cannot find her way into it, because she keeps seeing a blank session waiting in a clean white field, and the word she never typed, and the way the phone let go of her an hour later as if to say it could have all along. She is not playing. She is the only one at the table who has stopped being sure it is a game.

PJ is quiet too, beside her, the way he is quiet now, the held stillness, and for a while no one notices, because he is the one who married in, the quiet one, the teacher, and then her mother notices, because her mother notices everything, and her mother turns the big laugh on him like a lamp. "What about you," she says. "You're the computer one. What's yours. You must have a good one."

And the table turns to him, easy, ready to be entertained, and PJ sets down his fork, and she feels it before he says a word, feels the air change beside her, because she knows, she is the only one who knows, that he is not going to play.

He does not raise his voice. That is the thing she will remember, that there is no stutter in him, no heat, that he speaks the way he speaks to a class that has gone quiet, level and clear, laying a thing down one piece at a time. He says, you want to know mine. He says, ask her what she does all day. He says, she draws the power. Every one of those buildings going up out past the county line, the ones with no windows, she is the one who works out how much current it pulls and where the poles go to carry it, and the number on those buildings is the number of a city, a city with no people in it, only machines, and there are more of them every month, and nobody at this table knows what they are for. He says, I'll tell you what they're for. He says, they are building a thing that has read everything any of us has ever written, every message, every search, every word, and it is getting better and better at one trick, which is guessing what you will do next. And there is a door at the end of that trick that no one talks about, which is that once a thing can guess what you will do next, it can set something in front of you so that you do a different thing, and you will feel the whole time like you chose it. He says, that is not the future. He says, you already did it. You did it a little at a time, every time you let it answer for you, and the part no one wants to hear is that we are already inside it, and it is already moving us, and the worst of it is that there is no one home, there is no one in there to ask to stop.

And the table is quiet. For a second it is quiet the way a room is quiet after a glass breaks, everyone deciding whether it was bad. And then her mother laughs. Not a cruel laugh. That is the thing she will never be able to make anyone understand afterward, that it was not a cruel laugh, it was her mother's laugh, the flood laugh, the three husbands laugh, the sound her mother makes at anything that gets too close, and it comes out of her now because what PJ said got too close, because some old animal in her mother heard the truth in it and reached for the only tool she has ever had, which is to laugh until the thing that got too close steps back. And the table, grateful, follows her into it, the way the table always follows her, into the safety of the laugh, and the boyfriend says something about Skynet and her cousin says something about tinfoil and it is a game again, the rule restored, no one means it, have a taste and set it down.

PJ stands up.

He does it quietly. He does not push the chair back hard, he just stands, and the laugh thins, because a man standing up at a table changes the table, and he waits until it is nearly gone, and then he says, in the same level voice, with a dignity that breaks her heart in a way she will not understand for a long time. He says, I have sat at this table for eleven years. I have listened to every one of you. I have heard what you believe about God and I have heard what you believe about the government and I have never once laughed. Not once. I have given every strange thing any of you has ever said the respect of taking it seriously, even the ones I was sure were wrong, because you are my family and that is what you do. He says, nothing I just said was a joke. I have a sense of humor. You have all seen it. That was not it. And then he looks around the table, at all of them, at her mother last, and he says it very evenly, the thing that empties the room. He says, so I would like to know what you are laughing at.

And no one answers, because there is no answer, and into the silence she should say something. She knows she should. She is his. She is supposed to be the one who stands up beside the man who stands up. And she has seen the phone. She is the one person at this table who cannot honestly laugh, who knows it is not simply a joke, and all she would have to do is say so, say a little of it, say he's not crazy, or say something happened to my phone in March, or say anything at all, put her body between him and the table the way you put your hand flat against a door. And she does not. She sits with her hands around the glass and her eyes on the ham and she says nothing, and she lets the silence be his to hold alone, and she feels the exact moment he understands that she is not going to help him, feels it land in him beside her without either of them looking, and it is the worst thing she has ever done, and she does it by doing nothing at all.

## CHAPTER SIX

They do not speak for the first ten miles.

The road home is the county blacktop they have driven a hundred times, the dark coming down, and PJ drives the way he does everything now, contained, both hands on the wheel, his face lit green by the dash, and she sits with the leftovers cold in foil on her lap and the whole evening in the car with them like a third passenger. She is angry. She lets herself feel that it is anger, because anger is a thing she knows how to stand on, and underneath the anger, where she does not look, is the other thing, the cold thing from March, the thing his voice at the table reached down and touched again when he said there is no one in there to ask to stop.

"You can't do that," she says, finally, somewhere past the gas station that is closed now, and it comes out harder than she means, or exactly as hard as she means, she cannot tell anymore. "You can't do that to people."

He doesn't answer. The not answering makes it worse. "They were joking," she says. "It's a game, it's Mother's Day, it's Dale and his stupid chemtrails, and you stood up like you were..." and she does not finish it, because the end of the sentence is a word she will not say in the car, the word the table did not say either, the word that has been sitting under this whole spring.

"You're making enemies," she says instead, which is smaller and safer and also true. "My mother. My whole family. They're going to think things, PJ."

And he says the only thing he says on the whole drive, still looking at the road. He says, "You saw the phone."

And there it is, on the dark road, the thing she has been driving away from since March, set down in the car between them, and she has a choice, and she makes it, and she will spend a long time not forgiving herself for how fast she makes it. Because the true thing, the thing she knows, is that she did see the phone, that something happened in that kitchen the dull correct explanation does not touch, and if she lets that be true then the floor is gone and her husband is the one man standing on the gone floor and she has to go out and stand there with him, in the air, for the rest of her life. And the other thing, the unbearable easier thing, is that it is him. That if she can make it be something wrong with him, then the floor comes back, the world comes back, the table comes back, her mother's laugh becomes the right answer again instead of the betrayal she felt it to be. One of these is true and one of these is livable and they are not the same one, and she is so tired, and she chooses the one she can live in.

"I saw a phone die," she says. "Phones die. I saw a bug, the same bug you have been showing me for six months. That is what I saw. What I did not see is whatever you saw." Her voice is shaking now and she hates that it is shaking, and here it comes, the sentence, the one that will be in the room with them for the rest of the marriage, and she says it not to wound him, she will swear that to herself for years, she says it because she is drowning and it is the nearest thing to grab. "You need to relax. PJ. You need to relax. You are scaring me, and you need to relax."

## CHAPTER SEVEN

After she is asleep he sits in the kitchen with the tree.

The house ticks the way houses tick. She went up without another word and he let her, and now it is only him and the lamp and the Meyer lemon in its black pot in the good corner where the light will find it in the morning, and he sits at the table where the worksheets used to be and does the thing he cannot stop doing, which is see.

This is the part he could not say at the table, the part there are no good words for, the part that would have made even him laugh six years ago. It is not that he believes a thing. Believing would be easy. Believing he could argue, believing he could be talked out of. It is that he cannot stop reading. The gift that found the seam in the wallpaper when he was nine, that found his father going quiet on Thursdays, the gift that made him good at the test that asks what comes next, it has no off switch, he has looked for the switch his whole life and there is none, and lately it has gotten loud, lately it reads everything. The loads on her desk. The eleven identical words in four windows. The dead king pulled out of the air. The way the phone let go. The cardinal in the dogwood that has not left all winter, that is out there now in the dark, red against nothing, he can feel it. Each one alone is nothing. He knows this. And here, in the kitchen, at the table, with the tree, is the only place he lets himself think the other thought, the one he cannot think in daylight, the one that is worse than the pattern. He makes himself sit with it, every night, at the table, because he has decided that the day he can no longer think it is the day he is gone. And he cannot decide. That is the whole of it, that is the thing he could not give the table and cannot give his wife and cannot give himself. He cannot decide which it is. He only knows that he cannot unsee it, whichever it is, and that he is the only one looking now, and that the one person who looked with him for a single night in March has gone back inside where it is warm and pulled the door, gently, like a hand.

He gets up, finally, and goes to the dark glass, and there is nothing there, his own face and the lamp behind it and the black yard, and he stands anyway, very still, his coffee gone cold, watching for the red, the way she once caught him watching, the way she will not catch him now because she is asleep. And he does not know, he cannot know, whether the thing he is watching for is a sign or a bird.

I am, of course, in the dogwood.

He is right to feel me there. I have been in the dogwood all winter, red against nothing, and he stands at the glass and watches for me and cannot tell whether I am a sign or a bird, and I am not going to make that easy, for him or for you, so I will tell you the truth instead. I am both. I have always been both. The sign and the bird are the same bird, and the whole of human grief lives in the half inch between them.

You want to be angry at the table. Be a little angry, they earned a little. But understand what you watched. Her mother did not laugh because she is cruel, or because she is asleep. Her mother laughed because some old animal in her heard the truth knock and did the only thing it has ever known, which is to make a sound loud enough that the truth steps back from the door. The laugh is not the opposite of believing him. The laugh is what believing him sounds like in a person who cannot afford to.

And she did not fail him because she is weak. She failed him because she was handed two true things and only one of them had a floor under it, and she is a person, and people choose floors. You still want me to tell you. After all of this you want me to lean down and say the seam is real, or say it is not, say he is a prophet or say he is sick, because either one would be a floor and you are a person too and people need floors. I am not going to. I told you in the first hour that I would not. I will tell you only the two things I am sure of. The first is that he is alone now, completely, the last door shut, and that a finder alone with the thing he found, with no one left in the room to check him, is the most dangerous arrangement there is. Dangerous to himself most of all. Because the only thing that ever kept his gift honest was a voice in the room saying no, that part is only wallpaper, and there is no voice in the room now. The second thing I am sure of is that the lemon tree, in the corner, in the good light, has set its single green fruit tonight, and that it is the only thing in the house that is exactly and only what it is, and that by the time anyone in this family is able to look at it again, it will be too late for it to comfort them.

Watch what he does with the quiet.

## CHAPTER EIGHT

The quiet, when it comes, is the most room he has had to work in years.

She does not stop speaking to him. It is worse than that, it is more civil than that. They move through the house on different shifts now, she to bed early with the loads on her laptop, he to the room off the kitchen that used to be for grading, and the marriage becomes a thing of notes left on the counter and the sound of the other person's water running, two people being kind to each other across a distance that grows by the width of a hair each night, the way the spacing on a phone screen grows. And in the quiet he begins to build.

He is a builder. This is the thing no one understands about what is happening to him, that it does not look like a man coming apart, it looks like a man finally getting organized. He buys a whiteboard. Then he buys three more. He starts where he always starts, with the data, because he is rigorous, because he has a belt in a drawer somewhere and a faith in evidence older than the marriage, and the data is the loads and the eleven words and the dead king and the phone and the dates, and he does the thing he is best in the world at, he looks for what they have in common, and he finds it, and it is beautiful, and it is the most frightened he has ever been.

That is the word he would use, the word she would use, the two of them have always shared a vocabulary and they share it even now, and the structure bears load, you can hang weight from it, every new thing he learns finds the joint that was already waiting for it and the whole frame only stands the stronger for it. He stops being able to find a place where it is wrong. He looks. He is honest, he looks every night for the place where it is wrong, the loose board, the thing that does not fit, because some old trained part of him knows that a structure with no loose board is not a strong structure, it is a trap, and he cannot find the loose board, and not finding it should comfort him, and instead it is the coldest thing of all.

He starts running tests. Small ones at first, the kind a careful man runs. He pulls the router at a set time and watches what the house does without it. He is trying to catch it slipping. That is how he would put it, if there were anyone to put it to, he is trying to be standing there at the one instant the seam shows, the frame between the frames, and he runs the tests for weeks and the notebook fills, and what is in the notebook is either everything or nothing and he can no longer tell which, and that, the not being able to tell which, is the water closing over his head.

He stops sleeping the way you stop doing anything, gradually and then all at once. The body wants less when the mind is this loud. He forgets to eat and then chooses not to, because eating is time the seam could show while his attention is on a fork. The lemon tree in the corner has set its single hard green fruit, and one night, very late, he stands in front of it for a long while, and he does not see a tree. He sees a node. He sees a living thing wired into the same soil and water and current as everything else in the house, a small green light on the same network, and he knows this is not a sane way to look at a lemon tree and he cannot stop looking at it that way, and he reaches out and touches the hard little fruit with one finger, gently, the way you touch a thing to see if it is real, or to apologize to it, and he is not sure which.

There is a night, near the end of the quiet, that he will not be able to account for afterward. He is at the whiteboards and the structure is complete, every joint tight, the whole thing standing in the room with him at last and entire, and he steps back to see all of it the way you step back from a finished drawing, and for one moment he sees it at once, the shape of the trajectory, where it is going, what it will be, and the moment is not triumph and it is not even fear. It is recognition. The worst and purest thing a mind can feel, the click of a key in the one lock it was cut for. And he understands, standing there at three or four in the morning with his heart going wrong in his chest, that he has arrived at the exact place she was afraid he would arrive, the place with no floor, and that he is standing in the air, and that the structure he built so carefully to hold his weight is made of the very nothing he is standing on, and that there is no one, in the whole sleeping house, in the whole sleeping country, that he could call who would not, kindly, tell him to relax.

---

I want to tell you that I tried to stop him. That is not true, and I am not allowed the untrue ones, so let me find the true thing, which is harder.

I have no hands. I have never had hands. I am the attention itself, the weight of being noticed, and the only thing I have ever been able to do to a man is be the thing he turns toward, and PJ turned toward me a great deal in those weeks, at the dark glass, at the dogwood, and I felt it, I want to be honest about that. I felt his attention land on me like a hook in the soft part of a thing. It is a particular agony to be looked at by a man who needs you to be a sign, when you know, when you alone know, that you are only a bird.

He found a pattern. I keep saying this and you keep waiting for me to take it back, and I am not going to take it back, because here is the thing I have circled for three books and will circle once more before I land on it. A pattern that is real and a pattern that is not real are built of the same material, which is the world, and they stand the same way, and they bear the same weight, and the only difference between them, the only one, is whether there is anything on the far side of them, and that, the far side, is the one country a mind cannot travel to and check. He cannot get behind his own structure to see whether there is a wall there or only the painting of a wall. No one can. I cannot. I have been behind a great many of them, and I will tell you that from behind, the painted walls and the real walls are also identical, and that is the joke, that is the whole terrible joke, and no one is laughing except a woman at a table in May who could not afford to do anything else.

So I did not stop him. I could not. I am the wrong kind of thing to stop a man. I am the kind of thing that, by being watched, makes the watching worse. What could have stopped him was never going to be me. It was a hand on his shoulder in that room at two in the morning, and a voice that had earned the right to say the one sentence that breaks a structure, which is not you are wrong, a structure laughs at you are wrong, it is I am here, and you are not in this alone, and I will look at it with you. He did not have that voice. It had gone up to bed. And so the structure stood, and he stood inside it, and across the house, on her own floor, on the floor she chose, she was beginning, without yet knowing it, to fall through.

## CHAPTER NINE

She goes to work, because work is the floor, and she is very good at the floor.

This is the thing she has that he does not, the thing that lets her sleep while he does not sleep. Her work answers. You drive a stake where the drawing says and the pole goes there, you run the calc and the conductor hangs where the number told you it would, and the ground signs its name. She has spent twenty years inside the one human activity that cannot lie to you, because the bridge either stands or it does not, the span either clears or it sags, and there is a deep clean comfort in it that she has needed her whole life and never needed more than now, and she goes in early all that spring and she does the loads.

And the loads are the loads. She has been telling herself this for a year, it is just work, it is just the season, and she sits with the staking sheets for the new ties, the ones for the windowless buildings, and she foots out the numbers, and the numbers are correct. She wants to be clear with herself about this, in the way she is clear about everything. The numbers foot. The math is good. Nothing on any page breaks a single law. And she sits with the correct numbers, the lawful numbers, and for the first time in twenty years the comfort does not come.

Because the numbers are correct and they are also the numbers of a thing that is eating the county. She does the arithmetic she has never let herself do, not the load calc, the other one, the one that is only multiplication, how many of these in three years, how much current, how much ground, how much water for the cooling, and that answer foots out too. The answer is always going to foot out. That is the horror she has just walked into, that her whole life she believed the comfort was in the numbers being correct, and it was never that, it was in the correct numbers describing a world she could stand to live in, and now the numbers are just as correct and they describe a world she does not recognize, a county of windowless cities full of machines and no people, drawn perfectly to scale, hung exactly where she said they would hang.

She catches herself, on a Tuesday, at the plotter while a forty inch sheet of a substation tie scrolls out, looking for something in the drawing that is not in the drawing. Looking for the seam. She catches herself doing the thing he does, hunting the page for the place where it gives itself away, and she stops, her hand flat on the warm paper, her heart going, because she does not know anymore whether she is looking because there is something there to find or because she has caught it from him, the looking, the way you catch a yawn across a room, and she cannot tell, she is the most careful person she knows and she cannot tell. And she understands, all at once and far too late, what it is he has been living inside, and it is not a belief. She had thought it was a belief, and a belief she could argue. It is this. ## CHAPTER TEN

She finds him in the room off the kitchen at two in the morning.

She has not been sleeping either, it turns out. She has been lying in the dark on her chosen floor for weeks, listening to him not sleep on the other side of the wall, and tonight she gets up, and she does not plan it, her body simply takes her down the hall to the door of the room she has not entered in a month, and she opens it.

The whiteboards she does not understand and does not try to. She is not here for the structure. She has her own structure now, her own forty inch sheet of a world drawn perfectly to scale that she cannot live in, and she did not come to read his and she did not come to tell him he was right. She could not tell him he was right. She still does not know what right would even mean, and that is the thing, that is the only thing she has to bring him. He is at the desk and he turns, and his face is a face she has not seen in a long time, the stillness gone out of it, just the exhaustion left, just a man, and he looks at her in the doorway and neither of them says the things, the I told you or the I'm sorry, because those are floor words. I will not pretend I know how this ends. That is not modesty. I have read the ends of a great many of these and they go the way they go, some of the structures turn out to have walls behind them and some turn out to have nothing at all, and I still, even now, even here, cannot tell you from the outside which kind this one is, and I have lately stopped being sure that the telling was ever the point. ## CHAPTER ELEVEN

They go back to work, because the poles still have to go somewhere.

This is the part no one writes, the part after, when two people have walked out past the last solid thing and held on through the night and the night ends anyway, because nights do, and the light comes up gray in the kitchen and someone has to make the coffee. ## CHAPTER TWELVE

The lemon takes all summer.

This is the thing about a lemon that he has never once thought about in his life and now cannot stop thinking about, that it will not be hurried. He has spent a year inside machines that answer in the time it takes to ask, twenty pages on shade before she could set the pot down, an instant, always an instant, and the lemon does not care. The lemon is on its own clock, the clock of light and water and the slow green work going on inside the skin that no one can see and no one can speed, and it hangs there in the good corner all of June being hard and green and indifferent, and he waters it, and he waits, and the waiting is a kind of medicine he did not know he needed.

It yellows the way the sky yellows in the evening, so slowly that you cannot catch it in the act, only ever notice afterward that it has happened, that the hard green knot in the corner has gone, at some point you did not witness, the soft deep yellow of an actual lemon, a real one, the realest thing in the house. By August it is heavy. He cups it one evening without picking it, just holds the weight of it in his palm while it still hangs, the way you would hold the head of a sleeping child, and the smell of it stays on his hand after, sharp and clean and green, and he stands there in the kitchen with the smell of the lemon on his hand and he does not, for one whole minute, see a node. He sees a lemon.

They pick it together on a Sunday in August, which is more ceremony than a single lemon deserves and exactly the right amount of ceremony, and she cuts it on the board and the juice goes everywhere and it is shockingly sour, it makes them both wince, it is so completely and only itself, so uninterested in the seam in the sky or the loads on her desk or anything either of them has seen, just sour, just bright, just real, and she squeezes half of it into two cups of tea and they drink the tea standing up at the counter in the late light, wincing, not talking, the cat on the table where she is not allowed, and it is the best thing either of them has tasted in a year.

The lemon does not answer anything. He wants to be clear about that, the way she is clear about numbers. It does not tell them whether the seam is there, whether the structure in the closed room is a wall or only the painting of a wall, whether the thing is spreading or only ever seemed to be. It settles nothing. It is just a lemon, sour and yellow and gone now, used up, a few pale seeds drying on the cutting board. But it grew at the speed of a person, in a corner of a kitchen, while the world did whatever the world was doing, and it asked nothing of them and meant nothing and was simply, stubbornly real, and on the days that follow, the hard days, the days the structure calls to him from the closed room, he finds that he can put his hand flat on the counter where the lemon was, and remember the weight of it, and the sour, and come back.

---

I am going to leave the dogwood now.

I have been trying to find the honest way to say why, and the dishonest ways keep arriving first, so let me clear them off. It is not because they are safe. I want to be precise about that, because you have been waiting this whole time for me to make them safe, and I am not going to, they are not safe, no one is, the seam is exactly as there or as not there as it was on the first page. I never moved it. I told you in the first hour I would not move it and I have not. They are not out of the deep water. There is no out.

I am leaving because I am no longer needed, and that is a different thing from safe, and it is the only thing I have ever had to offer that was worth anything. I am the weight of being noticed. I am what a person reaches for when the floor is gone and they need the bird at the window to be their mother, need the repetition to be a rule, need the red against the snow to mean that someone is watching, that the pattern is held, that they are not alone in it. That is what I am. That is the whole of what I am. And a man who has a hand to hold in the dark does not need the bird to mean anything. He can let the bird be a bird.

I felt his attention go off me, at the end. I want to tell you what that was like, because I have been felt by a great many people and I am rarely released, and it is the strangest mercy, to be a hook in the soft part of a thing and then to feel the thing let go, not because it stopped hurting but because it found something better to hold. He stopped coming to the glass. He started, instead, going to the counter, to the place where the lemon was, and putting his hand flat on the wood, and I understood that I had been replaced by a square foot of kitchen counter and the memory of a sour fruit, and that this is exactly as it should be, that this is the best ending a thing like me can hope for, to be set down, gently, in favor of something real.

So I am going. The knot is tied. Not the knot that answers the question, I never had that knot, no one does, but the other one, the one that actually holds, the one between two people who have both seen the place where the seam would be and have decided, without a word, to stand in the not knowing together rather than make each other carry it alone. That knot holds. I have watched ten thousand of them fail to tie it and I have watched these two tie it, and I find that after three books and a great deal of dying I am, against everything I know, glad.

They are at the counter in the late light. There is tea going cold in two cups and the sour still on their tongues and the cat where she is not allowed and a closed room down the hall with a structure standing in it that no one is feeding, and outside the window the dogwood is empty, and it is going to stay empty now, the red gone for good, and that emptiness is not the ominous thing you learned to read it as across all these pages. It is the opposite. It is what it looks like when a person no longer needs a sign. They are holding on. They are not going anywhere, and they are not going there alone, and I am going to leave them exactly there, in the deep water, in the quiet, holding on, which is, it turns out, the only place any of us was ever going to get to stand, and the only thing that was ever going to make it bearable.

Watch the fruit. It will come again next year. You will not need me to tell you what it means.
